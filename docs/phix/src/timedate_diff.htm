timedate_diff

Definition: include builtins\timedate.e

atom a = timedate_diff(timedate td1, timedate td2, integer term=0)
Description: Returns the difference in seconds between two dates. A positive value is returned when td1 is before td2.

A term of DT_SECOND, DT_MINUTE, DT_HOUR, DT_DAY, DT_MONTH, or DT_YEAR may be supplied to truncate the difference to a whole number of that period (see examples below). Note that DT_MONTH is treated as 30 days, and DT_YEAR uses 365.25 to calculate the whole number of years, but then multiplies by 365 to get back to seconds.
If term is zero (the default) then no such truncation is attempted.
If term is negative the result is neither truncated nor converted back to seconds.
pwa/p2js: Supported.
Comments: As with timedelta, the result is intended to be used as a parameter to adjust_timedate(), and it can also be supplied as an argument to elapsed(), in both cases only when term was not negative, or to be pendantic not -DT_SECOND.

Note that results from date() should either have been generated with bMsecs:=true or have their [DT_MSEC] entries set to zero before being passed to timedate_diff().
Examples: 
include builtins\timedate.e
timedate birthdate = parse_date_string("1/1/2000",{"D/M/YYYY"}),
             today = parse_date_string("23/11/10 3:2:1",{"Y/M/D h:m:s"})
printf(1,"You are %s old\n",{elapsed(timedate_diff(birthdate,today,DT_DAY))})
-- displays "You are 23 years, 45 weeks, 4 days old", vs (without a term):
printf(1,"You are %s old\n",{elapsed(timedate_diff(birthdate,today))})
-- displays "You are 23 years, 45 weeks, 4 days, 3 hours, 2 minutes and 1s old"
printf(1,"You are %d years old\n",trunc(timedate_diff(birthdate,today,-DT_YEAR)))
-- displays "You are 23 years old"
printf(1,"You are %d years old\n",round(timedate_diff(birthdate,today,-DT_YEAR)))
-- displays "You are 24 years old"

By passing a whole number of days to elapsed(), the result contains no left-over hours, minutes, or seconds.
Aside: elapsed() doesn't even attempt to speak months, due to their tendency to have 28/29/30/31 days.
It becomes your responsibility/choice to truncate or round the result from a negative term, if needed.
See Also: timedate, adjust_timedate, parse_date_string, elapsed
