Layout Management

The layout manager at the very heart of theGUI is the sole survivor of a truly epic sortie deep behind enemy lines, most of which is either still classified or just too embarrassing to repeat. Some aspects may stem from difficulties that simply evaporated during the switch from native to fully emulated controls. The following assumes you have briefly scanned the gallery of available controls, the rest of this page is all and only about positioning them relative to each other, within a gDialog() or headerless version of that.

Abstract Layout

Originally adopted from IUP and almost mandated by GTK/HTML, it is hard to imagine anything simpler or a need for additional complexity. Should you prefer a tedious pixel-counting exercise that breaks whenever a different font or screen size is used (as in WinAPI), that (concrete layout) is available via gCbox() and not further discussed here.

The main characteristic of the abstract layout approach is that otherwise invisible gHbox() and gVbox() containers define a heirarchy which arranges controls and/or nested subgroups of them horizontally and vertically respectively (see gSplit() for a quick visualisation).
Should a dialog be resizeable, something has to absorb any excess space/slack, those two invisible containers are often the best candidates. Interface element positioning can easily be fine-tuned using margins, gaps, and similar simple rules.

Sole Child

When a single visible element is the sole child of a container, and in particular a rather ill-advised (say) gDialog(gButton) as opposed to gDialog(gHbox({gButton})), the rule book is essentially thrown out the window and the child expanded to fill the entire container, often precisely as wanted for a gCanvas(), gGraph(), gList(), gTable(), gTree(), gFrame(), and gSplit(), but very unlikely on a gButton(), gDatePick(), gDropDown(), gProgressBar(), gText(), and quite probably several others. Of course that also happens intentionally by design on a gHbox() or gVbox() but since they aren't visible and have all the features/attributes needed to deal with it, that's fine.

The Hard Slam

A term I made up to describle what happens when a dialog is resized to or below the natural minimum, whereby controls glide up and left until they nestle against each other, but no further and specifically not drifting off-screen nor overlapping. [ChatGPT suggested Hard Constraints, Hard Minimum, Clamping / Clamped Movement, Collision or Boundary Detection, Bottoming Out, and Non Overlapping, none of which felt any better.] Arguably, perhaps, it should impose a default MINSIZE, or maybe scrollbars could (perhaps optionally) appear on the dialog itself, but that'll have to wait for another day.

Attributes

In practice the layout is perfectly well controlled by a surprisingly small number of entirely optional attributes:
  • SIZE: Allows the natural size to be overidden.
  • MINSIZE/MAXSIZE: Should be pretty self-explanatory.
  • EXPAND: Allows an element or container to grow, widthwise, heightwise, or both.
  • MARGIN: As explained in that link and shown below, between one and four values.
  • GAP/SPACE: (gH/Vbox only) Additional space between/around elements.
These often interact with each other, sometimes rather subtly, for instance a gH/Vbox typically only expands in a given direction when at least one child can expand that way. Quite often some trial and error is needed to find the perfect combination, but that's usually fairly quick and painless.

Window vs. Client sizes

Some controls also have a "client" size as well as the usual "window" SIZE, for example:


Naturally the decorations may subtly differ between backends, resulting in a different client size for a given window size, or vice-versa. While theGUI does the best it can to abstract that away, some cases may benefit from the use of gVersion(-1|2) to fine-tune for different backends. Note that even a headerless window has different window and client sizes, due to the 1-pixel border. Except for the {w,h} passed to a gCanvas()/REDRAW handler, theGUI only provides the outer window size; should you need the inner size of a dialog or frame then simply ask the sole child of those controls.

Natural sizes

Most visible elements have a natural size, the notable exceptions being gCanvas(), gGraph(), gList(), and gTree(), which need to be either given an explict user size or allowed to expand into some size explicitly specified on one of their parent containers. Apart from that, the sizes can easily be accumulated to give a complete dialog a perfectly reasonable natural size as well (not necessarily stored anywhere), and without the programmer having to specify any positions at all. Should some things look a little cramped, spacing around and between interface elements can easily be specified using the MARGIN and GAP attributes, and to a lesser/indirect extent SPACE (when no EXPAND nicks it first). Interface elements also have an EXPAND attribute to control whether/how they fill/react to their parent container having excess space (which may effectively disable SPACE handling).

Normalisation

gNormalise(gdx ids, atom_string direction="BOTH")                      -- gNormalize() is just a simple alias.
ids: a sequence(/group) of elements to be normalised (leaf-only).
direction: can also be "HEIGHT", "WIDTH", or single-character (HWB), or 1..3.

Sets the same(/max) width/height/both on a set of controls for alignment purposes.
Elements can be normalised widthwise in one set(/call) and heightwise in another.
It is probably worth saying a gNormalise() isn't really any different to a bunch of gSetAttribute(SIZE) calls,
therefore should this in any way not quite meet your specific needs my advice is to do precisely that by hand.
Also, epandable elements are naturally normalised in the cross orientation of any parent container, such that a gVbox grants expandable children the same width, and (as part of that process) the container "inherits" the max/sum of child sizes. Since containers generally obtain their sizes, that is when not explicitly specified, by summing the post-normalised sizes of their children, that pretty much rules them out, besides, as just stated, performing the explicit equivalent manually should not in practice be particularly difficult.

The HTML/CSS Box Model

You may recognise the above from the Browser Development Tools, theGUI uses a similar but slightly extended model:

The three main changes are:
Only gButton, gProgressBar, gText (ditto gDatePick, gDropDown), and a gFrame (slightly indented at the top) actually need a border, and to be perfectly blunt I cannot see any practical benefit in allowing a programmer to play silly monkeys with those specific versions of a border.
(Besides, anyone sufficiently motivated can easily add a tweaked and enhanced copy of any of the existing standard controls anyway.)
Secondly, there does not want to be any padding on a progress bar, a gFrame can rely on the margin of its sole child, and the other four are better handled by auto-centering the content within an overidden SIZE anyway, hence (and to get it out the door a bit quicker) theGUI does not actually/currently have any border or padding settings. The upshot of all that is the layout manager only has to deal with/worry about margins, with gaps being pretty much the same thing/implemented as such in theGUI.js, which makes my life at least much simpler.
Third and lastly, a gap between the elements in a gH/Vbox can be specified along with a five-way mechanism for distributing any excess space when (and only when) there are no expanding children that absorb it first. The five settings on the right show how horizontal slack may be distributed on a gHbox, with (in green) that vertical slack may be applied to the TOP and/or BOTTOM of those children that do not themselves expand vertically. I'm sure you can imagine the equivalent diagram for a gVbox. Note that in the cross-orientation, BETWEEN is meaningless and treated as RIGHT/BOTTOM, whereas AROUND causes identical effects to CENTRE.

The SIZE does not include any margins, which are always taken care of by the parent container. Should you want to know the size of some inner blue bit, ask that child. The margin (pastel orange part) as well as any gaps/space is displayed in the BGCLR of the container whereas the contents (blue/green) that of the child elements, or at least ostensibly initialised to that, and all canvas/image/redraw handler sizes reflect that. The margins, along with any border/padding (of the implicit and non-overrideable kind), are not necessarily the neat rectangle shown, but may well turn out to be rather more blocky and bar-chart-like.

Garbage?

The layout manager is responsible for initially determining some sizes, and also swings into action when a dialog is manually resized, expanding and arranging elements as needed to fully occupy it, hopefully in an aesthetically pleasing manner. Note the accumulated sizes may also (one day) form a natural MINSIZE for the dialog, and setting a dialog size that otherwise seems unnecessary may still be of benefit when the top right "restore down" middle button is clicked, especially when the dialog is initially shown maximised.

One common issue is determining the ideal default initial size for a dialog. One trick is after manually resizing a dialog, executing a suitably placed ?gGetAttribute(dlg,`SIZE`) shows what you want, or use <Alt Prt Scr> to take a window snapshot then paste into mspaint to see what it says in the statusbar. Of course theGUI should always give you something fairly reasonable, but expecting it to deliver "the most aesthetically pleasing in context" as a default would be a bit extreme. Note there isn't yet an equivalent for IupConfigDialogShow() [maybe desktop-only]. You'll notice on(/off) Elements I've made them all 240x80 to line up nicely, just as an example or 21 and a bit of a "yeah, even me too" thing.

Overall, this approach requires relatively little effort on the part of the programmer, needing very little if any tedious pixel counting.

Since the layout is only evaluated on initial display and resize, leisurely performance would in fact be perfectly acceptable, and fretting over 0.1s, or in practice significantly less, would be utterly pointless. Breaking the problem down into several small and well-defined steps should make things much easier to maintain and extend, even though the overall process may not be easy to grasp at a glance, and it would be completely counter-productive to merge any of the stages detailed below.
  • tg_lm_get_dialog_decoration_size() (reasonably likely to need minor tweaks)
  • tg_lm_accumulate_sizes() (containers only, initial/sum/max [SZ_W/H] plus margin+gap)
  • tg_lm_apply_space() (for next, implements the SPACE attribute on h/vbox)
  • tg_lm_disperse_user_sizes() (final/expanded [SZ_W/H/X/Y], applies/respects margin+gap)
  • tg_lm_dump_ctrls() (debug aid)

Implementation (in theGUI.e for the GTK and WinAPI backends)

The layout manager works by juggling a whole heap of sizes: natural, user, min, max, normalised, and final, and applying margins, initially accumulating sizes up the hierarchy, and then distributing spacing/expansion downwards. Much of the code is a little vague and/or experimental, rather than adhering to some clear easily digestible overall design, since there isn't one - these docs are the closest you'll get to that. The reality is my efforts to come up with a proper design certainly helped to simplify things a great deal at first, but beyond a certain point started going in circles, so after that I just started trying to use it, and fixing the many inevitable "bugs" as and when they got in the way. Should you want to study the layout management code, the relevant (local) routines in theGUI.e all begin with "tg_lm_"/are the only ones that contain "lm", and are all post-mapping:
  • tg_lm_get_dialog_decoration_size() (fudged under GTK)
  • tg_lm_set_element_sizes() (set [SZ_NATURAL_W/H], elements only, no margins)
  • tg_lm_gather_normal_groups() (for next)
  • tg_lm_normalise_sizes() (set [SZ_NORMAL_W/H])
  • tg_lm_accumulate_sizes() (initial/sum/min [SZ_W/H], containers only, adds margin+gap)
  • tg_lm_apply_space() (for next, implements the SPACE attribute on h/vbox)
  • tg_lm_disperse_user_sizes() (final/expanded [SZ_W/H/X/Y], applies/respects margin+gap)
  • tg_lm_apply_sizes_and_offsets() (apply [SZ_W/H/X/Y])
  • tg_lm_dump_ctrls() (debug aid)
Those routines operate on the various internal sizes:
Natural: Note that only the "leaf" elements have a natural size (gButton, gCheckbox, gDatePick, gDropDown, gFrame, gLabel, gProgressBar, gSlider, gSpin, gTabs, gText, gTable), and consistency is more important than accuracy, besides it is not possible to actually measure (say) the invisible margin some backend decides a text label has. Very likely to differ between backends.
Note the natural size of a gFrame is "the text size" rather than "the container size".
Normal: the natural sizes after normalisation.
User: as explicitly supplied by the programmer, if any, ditto min/max.
Final: aka SZ_W/H/X/Y.
See just before theGUI.e/g_log(), approx line 2350, for some ways to produce logs of what is going on, from what/when the internal tables are being updated to exactly what you are asking the underlying backend to do, and what it is saying back. Naturally it will probably require extending on each use, but hopefully without reinventing the wheel every time.

Be warned that layout management is by far the trickiest part of theGUI and everything it does must have a CSS equivalent / apply to the box model as shown above (that list is probably more for my benefit than yours). Getting tg_lm_dump_ctrls() to show that you have calculated everything properly (esp natural sizes) is just the first step: getting WinAPI and GTK to obey you is altogether another matter entirely. As said, HTML/CSS does things differently, and to be honest about it, the sole (canvas) child case is really the only thing I (personally) care much about, at least as yet. As mentioned above, there are a fair few places where hard-coded adjustments/fudges are used, that probably deserve to be replaced with the "proper calculation via API", whatever that might actually be. My attempts to break the task down into eight smaller/simpler pieces (plus one diagnostic routine) have not really helped. There is also an otherwise undocumented gSetBodge() debug aid that I found helpful, at least initially, and which may one day become the basis for some much-needed unit/regression testing (on desktop/Phix only?).

While (hopefully) this may make little sense right now, the natural sizes are in fact the most critical aspect of the layout manager: without natural sizes you simply cannot display anything naturally. Being out by even a single pixel is pretty much guaranteed to find a way to accumulate across or down the screen to something pretty ugly pretty fast. It is not helped when a backend (esp GTK3) is obstinately opinionated about such matters and simply ignores anything it deems "too small". What I have tended to do is paste a screenshot, or three dozen, into mspaint, clip, zoom in, and display a grid, then carefully count pixels. The ugliest parts of theGUI.e are where such efforts have resulted in ad-hoc calculations and little constants scattered seemingly at random throughout that source, and even worse, in different places for different backends.

Note there is no intention to achieve pixel-perfect consistency between different backends, since they have different window decorations and fonts, and even GTK2 and GTK3 on the same box are visually rather (rudely/arrogantly) distinct. Besides, should you display a screenshot of a Windows program on a Linux desktop, or vice versa, it might look a bit odd and out-of-place, and you are probably more likely to want to hide the standard window decorations in a browser than on the desktop. Things should however be reasonably similar and more importantly perfectly/intuitively use-able. That said, there is no prohibition on or expectation you will never need minor platform-specific tweaks, and at least some uses/tests of gUseGTK() on Windows do actually attain pixel perfection, albeit often with subtly different background colours.
